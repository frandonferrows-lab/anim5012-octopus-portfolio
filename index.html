Portfolio Overview

This website presents husbandry guidance for the Giant Pacific Octopus, with a focus on diet, preventative health and enclosure design. The aim is to provide clear and practical recommendations for professional aquarists, keepers, curators and veterinarians who may already understand animal care, but are less familiar with the specific needs of this species.

How to use this website: The pages below are organised into the three main areas of the portfolio. Each section is designed to be quick to navigate, with tables and practical guidance that can be used in a professional aquarium setting.

Diet & Nutrition

Covers natural feeding behaviour, proposed captive diet, nutrient considerations, feeding schedules and food intake monitoring.

Preventative Health

Outlines the main health risks in captivity, including water quality problems, injury risks, infectious disease, quarantine and routine monitoring.

Enclosure Design

Provides recommendations for enclosure layout, water parameters, life support systems, secure design, cleaning access and animal safety.

Species Profile

Figure 1. Giant Pacific Octopus (Enteroctopus dofleini). Image source: ©Ashley Christiano.

Table 1. Species profile and captive management considerations.

Scientific name Enteroctopus dofleini
Natural range The Giant Pacific Octopus is found across the North Pacific Ocean, including coastal waters around Japan, Russia, Alaska, British Columbia and the west coast of North America. It is mainly associated with cold, temperate marine environments.
Habitat In the wild, this species usually lives close to the seabed in rocky reefs, kelp forests, crevices and dens. It may occur from shallow coastal areas to much deeper waters, although captive management normally focuses on stable, cold-water conditions that reflect its coastal marine habitat.
Relevant captive management concerns The Giant Pacific Octopus is highly intelligent, physically strong and capable of escape if an enclosure is not secure. In captivity, particular attention is needed for water quality, temperature stability, safe enclosure fittings, den availability and close monitoring of feeding behaviour. Its short lifespan and natural senescence also mean that changes in appetite, behaviour and activity must be recorded carefully.
